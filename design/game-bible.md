# NOT HERE — Game Bible

> Canonical design reference. Every scene, line, cue, and mechanic must be consistent
> with this document. The twist is LOCKED — see `twist-recontext-table.md` before
> writing any scene. Setting spec (wins all conflicts): `retheme-lorn-bay.md`.
> Decisions log: `decisions.md`. Full research: `research/`.

## One sentence

Seven years after you drowned, you walk back into the town that never stopped
grieving you — but you are not who they think you are, and neither is the grief.

## Premise

Lorn Bay, BC: an invented community on the west side of Okanagan Lake, year-round
population ~300, forty minutes from Penticton ("town"). Summer place — paddleboards,
tasting rooms, a packed beach in July. In November it is fog, frost fans, and eleven
lit windows. The lake fog inversion sits on the water for weeks. **Now: early
November, present day. Day 1 = Nov 6.**

The lake ferry quit when the highway went through, but its compressed-air horn still
stands at the end of the old wharf, and every night at 3:12 AM it plays five bars of
a melody out over the water — five bars, then silence where a sixth should be.
Nobody in Lorn Bay wakes for it anymore.

You wake on the gravel beach below the breakwater light, "soaked," and walk into a
town that greets you as a miracle: Wren Cole, Dianne's daughter, guitar player,
drowned past the breakwater seven years ago this month — canoe found overturned,
body never recovered. The lake is deep and cold; it keeps what it takes; everyone
knows a story like it. But the welcome is wrong in a hundred small ways. Your mother
weeps and holds you and never once says your name. Barb's old dog walks through the
space where you stand. Your little brother says, to your face, "You're not my
sister," and no one in the diner will meet his eye.

## The Twist (LOCKED)

**Wren never drowned. She left.**

- Sam (11 then) saw her go and was sworn to silence.
- Tam drove her out on the 4:10 AM Penticton run; his mileage log reads *one
  passenger, cash, no name — 04:10* — the only paper proof she lived.
- Dianne found the goodbye note the next morning and burned it, choosing "drowned"
  over "she left us."
- Priya received Wren's real goodbye letter ("I can't stay where everyone has
  already decided who I am") and never answered it.
- Wade argued with her that last night, heard her at the horn at 3:12, and didn't
  come down. He has played her unfinished tune on the horn nightly for seven years
  as penance.

**You are not Wren returned.** You are what seven years of calling finally called:
the town's remembering, condensed by fog into a body — assembled from five people's
curated private memories of her. You know the song but not its sixth bar (she
finished it after she left, after the memories you're made of end). Each person
finds a different flaw in your face because each compares you to their own private
Wren. The dog cannot see you. No one ever touches you first.

**The trigger:** three weeks before Day 1, Dianne got a letter with no return
address. Wren is alive, and she is coming home on the Friday bus — the EBUS from
Vancouver via Penticton, **Friday, Nov 28** (Day 23), the date circled twice on the
schedule card on the General's corkboard, pen pressed through. Tam does the pickup:
the man who drove her out is booked to drive her home. The town's guilt-panic
condensed the fog into you: a rehearsal-Wren, a confession they could practice on,
a version who might forgive.

**The double edge:** they are protecting you from knowing what you are — and
protecting *her* from you, because every memory you absorb overwrites the true one.
If you are fully "here" when the real Wren steps off that bus, no one left in Lorn
Bay will recognize the woman they actually lost.

## The digital absence (modern-mystery doctrine)

Phones exist. They make it worse:

1. **You have no phone.** Nobody offers you one. Nobody remarks on it.
2. **Photos of you disagree.** Every phone photo of you looks fine to whoever took
   it and subtly wrong to everyone else — each person's camera catches *their* Wren.
3. **Your voice doesn't record.** Sam's clips play back with room tone where you
   spoke. He shows no one. Who would believe the file wasn't doctored?
4. **Wren's accounts went dark seven years ago.** A memorial page exists; Dianne has
   never once posted to it. Three weeks ago she stopped opening it.
5. **Signal is thin in the Bay** ("the bench eats it") — real interior-BC normal,
   not a conspiracy; the town runs on the corkboard and the diner.

The instruments refuse to corroborate you; only people do. That asymmetry is the
doctrine's whole point, and every scene that touches a screen must honor it.

## Player character

- Female, explicit. Second person, present tense, spare and close. She has Wren's
  hands, most of her face, five bars of her song.
- The save name typed at start is never spoken by any resident ("love," "hon,"
  "kiddo," "the guest") — until the endings where it matters.
- Character creation is the **Counter Interview**: Barb pours decaf and asks five
  strange questions; answers seed stats and three self-truth tags (see
  `llm-touchpoints.md` §1) plus a musical fingerprint hidden in the main theme from
  the title screen.
- The player and protagonist share the same epistemic state at all times: you learn
  what you are exactly when she does.

## Cast

### Dianne Cole — mother; Lorn Bay General
Runs the General: store, Canada Post counter, community corkboard, the till drawer.
Wants her daughter back and for no one to look too closely at the miracle. Burned
the goodbye note; got the second letter three weeks ago. **Never says "Wren" to
you** — "love," "hon," "my girl" — unless you take her lullaby memory (bars 1–2);
the morning after, she calls you Wren for the first time *and it lands like a door
closing* (she can no longer hum it herself). Press her about the disappearance 3+
times and she starts locking the house at night; the game quietly stops letting you
enter without knocking.

### Wade Pike — caretaker of the old ferry wharf; the score's source
Last engineer of the lake ferry. The district pays him to maintain the breakwater
light; nobody pays him for the horn. He keeps it fed from the compressor shed and
plays her unfinished tune nightly at 3:12 as penance — the last to "see Wren
alive." **Knows the calling made you and keeps playing anyway** — you are his
forgiveness made flesh. Tracks what you hum: stay inside bars 1–5 and he weeps and
confesses early; hum an original sixth bar at him and he goes white ("she never
finished it") and locks the compressor shed — you've proven you aren't her, and his
Act 3 confession must be earned another way.

### Dr. Priya Anand — clinic; Wren's best friend and almost-lover
Runs the two-day-a-week clinic; came back for it. The antagonist-shaped character
who is right about everything. Wants to prove you're an impostor — and to be wrong.
Holds Wren's real goodbye letter, unanswered; her forensic hostility is displaced
guilt. **Her notebook is genuinely tracked by the engine**: quote your own Day-3
words back on Day 14 and she softens a trust tier; contradict an established detail
and she circles it in red at the potluck. Use a memory only Dianne gave you
privately and she catches the seam: "You weren't there for that. Dianne was. So how
do you carry it?"

### Sam Cole — brother; 11 then, 18 now
The only person who says the true thing out loud. Saw Wren leave; promised; has
been the crazy one for seven years. Hunts proof with his phone — and his recordings
of you play back as room tone, which he shows no one. Tests you with trap questions
(misquotes a private joke to see if you "correct" him with harvested detail).
High-UNDERTOW players get the game's most guarded friendship: "You're not her.
Fine. Then who are you? Because I could use somebody who isn't pretending."

### Barb Kettle — the Kettle; keeper of the register
Diner room plus six motel units out back; "Barb's" to locals; her coffee is the
coffee. Keeps the register and tab book by hand. Knew from Day 1 (her husband "came
back" for one winter, forty years ago) and said nothing, because that winter was
the best of her life. Wants you to have one good winter; nothing more, because she
knows there isn't more. **Warns you exactly once.** Her register is the diegetic
save file: entries about you fade overnight unless she double-inks them. Late Act 2
she reads your own history back to you, including choices you'd forgotten. NG+:
"Sugar in your coffee, wasn't it? Last winter."

### Tam Osei — regional bus driver
Penticton runs; the only one who regularly leaves. Keeper of the mileage log — the
single paper proof Wren lived (*one passenger, cash, no name — 04:10*). Watches how
you treat Sam: defend Sam at the potluck and Tam shows you the log unprompted;
sacrifice Sam and Tam's route "gets rescheduled" until Act 3. The escape hatch: the
Stranger ending leaves on his 4:10 AM run, in the same seat she took. Moose, Barb's
ancient lab-cross, waits at the diner door for his last run every night.

## Structure — 23 days, 3 acts

**Act 1 — The Return (Days 1–7).** Arrival, reunions, Counter Interview, the town's
story. Core loop: day/night scene slots (Presence Economy — see below), gathering
memories raises ECHO at visible cost. **Hard branch — the Foghorn Choice (Night
7):** catch Wade playing your tune at 3:12 on the wharf. Let him keep calling
(stats stay fed; complicity) or make him stop (all presence stats begin nightly
decay — a survival clock; only road to Stranger endings). Splits Act 2 staging.

**Act 2 — The Seams (Days 8–19).** Priya's notebook fills; Sam hunts proof; the ash
in Dianne's woodstove tin, Tam's mileage log, the circled schedule card. **Midpoint
set piece — the memorial potluck** (community hall, out-of-tune upright piano):
Sam stands up in front of everyone: "That is not my sister." Verdict computed from
accumulated FLESH+NAME witness: defended (Sam ostracized — a cost you carry) or
half-exiled to the wharf, bunking in the old ticket office (different Act 3
staging). **Hard branch — the Letter:** the trail ends at the till drawer of Lorn
Bay General. Open it (knowing route) or burn it unread (willful innocence; the
reveal ambushes you at the bus stop). Dark exit: burn Barb's register instead →
Ash ending.

**Act 3 — The Friday Bus (Days 20–23).** Confession cascade: each character can be
brought to hand over their piece of the truth; each confession returns that
character's musical fragment to the mix. Then Friday, Nov 28: the wharf, the
highway pull-in above it, the EBUS door hissing open. Endings resolve from stat
profile × Foghorn Choice × Letter Choice × confessions.

### Presence Economy (hard scarcity)
Each day offers 2–3 slots from a larger simultaneous set; ~4 of 6 confession paths
are reachable per run. Missed scenes resolve as authored **"without-you"
retellings** — biased secondhand accounts — and their motifs play faint and detuned
under the scenes you chose. NPCs track attendance ("you were out at the wharf when
Dianne needed the stockroom shelves"). FOMO calibration rule: absence must produce
*good content*, tutorialized by an early warm retelling.

### Fading is real
Presence collapse (post-Foghorn-stop decay unmanaged) triggers **Unwitnessed**
mid-Act-3. Generous autosave: reload lands 1–2 nights back.

## Endings (7, all full strength)

1. **The Sixth Bar** (true) — high UNDERTOW, all six confessions, compose an
   original sixth bar and play it on the horn as the bus pulls in above the wharf.
   Greet the real Wren by name; tell the town the truth; dissolve at the wharf's
   edge — but the horn keeps your bar. Devastating grace.
2. **Wren, Again** — high ECHO, low UNDERTOW, potluck defended, letter burned. You
   pass. The real Wren steps off the bus and *no one recognizes her*; she climbs
   back on before the doors close, and the bus pulls away while the town waves at
   you. Quiet horror; achievement reads "Welcome Home."
3. **Two Wrens** (hidden) — letter opened, all confessions, high FLESH/NAME, ECHO
   deliberately capped: refuse the two harvests that erase (Dianne's lullaby,
   Priya's letter-memory). Wren reads the bus stop in one look and **takes your
   hand — the game's first unprompted touch.** Uncanny hope.
4. **The Stranger on the Gravel** — horn stopped, high UNDERTOW, Sam alliance;
   leave on Tam's 4:10 AM run under the name you gave Barb, in Wren's seat, before
   the Friday bus arrives. Headlights on frost. Seeds NG+ hardest.
5. **The Long Winter** — mid stats, Barb's trust maxed, her counsel taken: one
   winter, no more. You choose your last night; Wade plays you out at 3:12; Barb
   double-inks your final entry so the book keeps you.
6. **Unwitnessed** — presence collapse. The game continues without you for four
   minutes — characters talking past your dead prompt, a register entry unfinished
   mid-sentence — then the interface idles at a cursor that accepts no input.
7. **Ash** — burn Barb's register in Act 2. It works: the town forgets Wren
   entirely — you, her, both. Dianne holds the guitar and asks Sam whose it is;
   Sam, promise horribly voided, doesn't know.

## Music — the score IS the mystery

**One broken song is the entire soundtrack.** "The Foghorn Song": six bars in
D dorian; bar 6 is a rest. Everything derives from bars 1–5; the game is,
musically, the act of reassembly, and the player finishes it or fails to. The song
is Wren's — she wrote it on the guitar; the horn learned it from Wade.

| Character | Fragment | Voice |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Dianne | bars 1–2 | music-box lullaby over harmonium |
| Wade | foghorn drone + bar 3 | the horn itself, nightly at 3:12 |
| Priya | bar 4 **inverted** | out-of-tune community-hall upright; her confession un-inverts it live |
| Sam | bar 5 | whistled fast, syncopated, the way an 11-year-old sped it up |
| Barb | accompaniment chords only | never presumed to know the tune; harmonizes with everyone |
| Tam | pitchless rhythm | road-and-engine ostinato; the pulse that carried Wren out |

Mechanics: CHORD is the tracklist; STATIC detunes/bitcrushes live; ECHO adds
Wren's melodic material; UNDERTOW reharmonizes toward a new mode (the player's
candidate sixth bars haunt the underscore before they consciously compose one).
Each Act 3 confession returns a fragment to the ensemble. The reveal plays the
first complete bars 1–5 heard in the game, then holds the rest where bar 6 should
be, foghorn distant.

Adopted grafts: the **player's counter-interview fingerprint hidden (inverted) in
the main theme from the title screen**; **lie-detune** (a lying character's stem
drops a quarter-tone — CLARITY/EAR-gated listening skill, always paired with a
visual twin); **tempo-as-dread** (a fixed bpm the ear learns to associate with time
running out, deployed at the wharf in every ending).

Palette: chiptune-folk hybrid — triangle music box, FM harmonium/foghorn, pulse
fiddle, noise-channel sea, tape warble. The terminal chip rendition is canon: "the
same town heard through a thinner wall."

Note data, arrangements, and timbres are untouched by the retheme; only cue names
and export identifiers move (`dianne-theme`, `FRAGMENT_DIANNE`, etc.).

## Prose grammar (invariants — mechanically linted)

1. **No one ever touches you first.** Every touch in the prose is initiated by you
   ("you take her hand," "you lean into the hug"). Broken exactly once, in the
   endings that break it deliberately (Two Wrens; variants at the bus stop).
2. **Dianne never says the player-facing name** ("love," "hon," "my girl") until
   the lullaby-taken flag.
3. **The bus date is on screen from Day 1** (beneath the Kettle register on
   Night 1, repeated on the General's corkboard; both builds).
4. The Day 1 arrival text states the wrongness as fact: no lake smell, no milfoil
   in your cuffs, hair dry at the roots, **no cold in you** — on a November night
   that would kill a swimmer in forty minutes.
5. The dog **never growls** — Moose fails to register you.
6. **No one ever remarks that anything is strange.** The town narrates around the
   wrongness; so does the prose.

## Prose register

Spare, modern, second person, present tense. Weather as fact, absence as lens.
Contractions in dialogue. No antique cadence — the Bay runs on debit taps, a tab
book, and a corkboard; Tim Hortons exists but Lorn Bay is too small for one, so
Barb's coffee is the coffee. Budget per `decisions.md`: 30–90 words per beat.

## LLM touchpoints (4 — classification/formatting only)

The LLM reads the register; it never writes canonical state. Outputs are cached in
saves and never regenerated. The no-key game is complete and good.

1. **Counter Interview** — free text → stat weights + 3 self-truth tags (fixed
   24-tag taxonomy), echoed verbatim in authored scenes. Fallback: multiple choice.
2. **Sixth Bar judging** (3 moments) — free-text attempt → enum borrowed/new.
   Fallback: notated variants pre-tagged.
3. **Priya's Interrogation** (one scene) — answer vs fact-ledger → PASS /
   CONTRADICTION / EVASION. Fallback: dialogue-tree trap.
4. **The Last Letter** (endings 1, 3, 5) — copy-edit player's letter to the house
   register (spare, modern), content unchanged. Fallback: authored letters.

Access: AIAdapter with three drivers — none / BYOK / edge proxy (spend-capped,
degrades to deterministic).

## NG+ / cross-run (full diegetic)

- Barb's register persists as a **real plain-text file beside the saves**; players
  can open it in any editor; Barb reacts if it's been edited.
- Prior run save-names surface faintly (a faded name in the tab book).
- A kid on the beach hums your previous run's composed sixth bar — your last self
  is town folklore now. ("How many winters? How many yous?")
- Browser and terminal share the save file; Barb acknowledges players who open
  "both doors."

## Tone & boundaries

Truly dark permitted — quiet horror, erasure, dissolution — inside warm domestic
texture (washed dishes, the diner at closing, morning coffee). No gore; nothing
gratuitous. Landing-page content note: grief, memory loss, death of a sibling,
ambiguous self-dissolution. Accessibility: full visual parity — every audio tell
has a first-class visual twin; all 7 endings reachable silent; reduced-flicker
mode.
