# NOT HERE — Game Bible

> Canonical design reference. Every scene, line, cue, and mechanic must be consistent
> with this document. The twist is LOCKED — see `twist-recontext-table.md` before
> writing any scene. Decisions log: `decisions.md`. Full research: `research/`.

## One sentence

Seven years after you drowned, you walk back into the town that never stopped
grieving you — but you are not who they think you are, and neither is the grief.

## Premise

Port Lorn: a fog-locked ferry town on a tidal causeway, British coast, spring 1971.
Off-season. The tide timetable is scripture; the pub fire is the town's only public
warmth; the lighthouse foghorn plays five bars of a melody every night at 3:12 AM —
five bars, then silence where a sixth should be.

You wake on the shingle below the light, "soaked," and walk into a town that greets
you as a miracle: Wren Halloway, church-organist's daughter, fiddle player, drowned
off the lighthouse seven years ago tonight. But the welcome is wrong in a hundred
small ways. Your mother weeps and holds you and never once says your name. Your
childhood dog walks through the space where you stand. Your little brother says, to
your face, "You're not my sister," and no one in the pub will meet his eye.

## The Twist (LOCKED)

**Wren never drowned. She left.**

- Sam (11 then) saw her go and was sworn to silence.
- Tam drove her across the causeway at 4 AM; his log reads *one passenger, name
  withheld by request* — the only paper proof she lived.
- Dora found the goodbye note the next morning and burned it, choosing "drowned"
  over "she left us."
- Ivy received Wren's real goodbye letter ("I can't stay where everyone has already
  decided who I am") and never answered it.
- Elias argued with her that last night, heard her at the foghorn at 3:12, and
  didn't come down. He has played her unfinished tune on the horn nightly for seven
  years as penance.

**You are not Wren returned.** You are what seven years of calling finally called:
the town's remembering, condensed by fog into a body — assembled from five people's
curated private memories of her. You know the song but not its sixth bar (she
finished it after she left, after the memories you're made of end). Each person
finds a different flaw in your face because each compares you to their own private
Wren. The dog cannot see you. No one ever touches you first.

**The trigger:** three weeks before Day 1, Dora received a letter. Wren is alive
and coming home on the spring ferry — the date circled on the post-office pinboard
from the first scene. The town's guilt-panic condensed the fog into you: a
rehearsal-Wren, a confession they could practice on, a version who might forgive.

**The double edge:** they are protecting you from knowing what you are — and
protecting *her* from you, because every memory you absorb overwrites the true one.
If you are fully "here" when the real Wren steps off that ferry, no one left in
Port Lorn will recognize the woman they actually lost.

## Player character

- Second person, present tense, spare and close.
- The save name typed at start is never spoken by any resident ("love," "pet,"
  "kiddo," "the guest") — until the endings where it matters.
- Character creation is the **Pub Interview**: Maud pours and asks five strange
  questions; answers seed stats and three self-truth tags (see `llm-touchpoints.md`
  §1) plus a musical fingerprint hidden in the main theme from the title screen.
- The player and protagonist share the same epistemic state at all times: you learn
  what you are exactly when she does.

## Stats — "in what way are you here?"

| Stat | Presence of | Gates | Fed by | Costs |
|------|-------------|-------|--------|-------|
| FLESH | Body | holding the fiddle, carrying a coffin handle, the dog seeing you, doors needing your hand | eating meals cooked for you, every touch you initiate | the cook's portion; the touched person's flinch |
| NAME | Record | signing the parish register, introductions to mainlanders, ledger entries surviving the night | being written about, vouched for, named aloud | the voucher's credibility; Maud's ink |
| ECHO | Memory (Wren's) | "you remember" dialogue, fastest trust | taking others' memories of Wren | **the giver loses that memory forever** |
| UNDERTOW | Self (not-Wren) | the truth-path, the pencil door, Sam's real friendship, the truest endings | refusing the mask: hating her tea, refusing the fiddle, telling truth | the town's fear; Ivy's notebook fills |
| CHORD (meter) | Music | mystery progress; **literally drives the adaptive mixer** | gathering song fragments via confessions | — |
| STATIC (meter 0–100) | The fog's claim | high STATIC locks out the best endings | rises: lying, forcing, looking away | detunes/bitcrushes the score live; glyph-rot corrupts your own dialogue options |

Rules: no stat is raisable without cost to a named person. Display is diegetic
only — a "consult the ledger" verb renders stats as Maud's handwritten
observations. Never show bare numbers.

## Cast

### Dora Halloway — mother; post office & tide-clock
Wants her daughter back and for no one to look too closely at the miracle. Burned
the goodbye note; received the second letter three weeks ago. **Never says "Wren"
to you** — "love," "pet," "my girl" — unless you take her lullaby memory (bars
1–2); the morning after, she calls you Wren for the first time *and it lands like a
door closing* (she can no longer hum it herself). Press her about the disappearance
3+ times and she starts locking the house at night; the game quietly stops letting
you enter without knocking.

### Elias Pike — lighthouse keeper; the score's source
The last to "see Wren alive." Plays her unfinished tune on the horn nightly at 3:12
as penance. **Knows the calling made you and keeps playing anyway** — you are his
forgiveness made flesh. Tracks what you hum: stay inside bars 1–5 and he weeps and
confesses early; hum an original sixth bar at him and he goes white ("she never
finished it") and locks the horn room — you've proven you aren't her, and his Act 3
confession must be earned another way.

### Ivy Anand — doctor; Wren's best friend and almost-lover
The antagonist-shaped character who is right about everything. Wants to prove you're
an impostor — and to be wrong. Holds Wren's real goodbye letter, unanswered; her
forensic hostility is displaced guilt. **Her notebook is genuinely tracked by the
engine**: quote your own Day-3 words back on Day 14 and she softens a trust tier;
contradict an established detail and she circles it in red at the Wake. Use a
memory only Dora gave you privately and she catches the seam: "You weren't there
for that. Dora was. So how do you carry it?"

### Sam Halloway — brother; 11 then, 18 now
The only person who says the true thing out loud. Saw Wren leave; promised; has
been the crazy one for seven years. Tests you with trap questions (misquotes a
private joke to see if you "correct" him with harvested detail). High-UNDERTOW
players get the game's most guarded friendship: "You're not her. Fine. Then who are
you? Because I could use somebody who isn't pretending."

### Maud Kettle — landlady of the Kettle & Anchor; keeper of the ledger
Knew from Day 1 (her husband "came back" for one winter, forty years ago) and said
nothing, because that winter was the best of her life. Wants you to have one good
winter; nothing more, because she knows there isn't more. **Warns you exactly once.**
Her ledger is the diegetic save file: entries about you fade overnight unless she
double-inks them. Late Act 2 she reads your own history back to you, including
choices you'd forgotten. NG+: "Sugar in your tea, wasn't it? Last winter."

### Tam Osei — causeway bus & ferry man
Came in '58; the only one who regularly leaves. Keeper of the route log — the
single paper proof Wren lived. Watches how you treat Sam: defend Sam at the Wake
and Tam shows you the log unprompted; sacrifice Sam and Tam's route "gets
rescheduled" until Act 3. The escape hatch: the Stranger ending leaves on his bus,
in the same seat she took.

## Structure — 23 days, 3 acts

**Act 1 — The Return (Days 1–7).** Arrival, reunions, Pub Interview, the town's
story. Core loop: day/night scene slots (Presence Economy — see below), gathering
memories raises ECHO at visible cost. **Hard branch — the Foghorn Choice (Night
7):** catch Elias playing your tune at 3:12. Let him keep calling (stats stay fed;
complicity) or make him stop (all presence stats begin nightly decay — a survival
clock; only road to Stranger endings). Splits Act 2 staging.

**Act 2 — The Seams (Days 8–19).** Ivy's notebook fills; Sam hunts proof; the ash
in Dora's stove tin, Tam's log, the circled ferry date. **Midpoint set piece — the
Anniversary Wake:** Sam stands on a table: "That is not my sister." Verdict
computed from accumulated FLESH+NAME witness: defended (Sam ostracized — a cost you
carry) or half-exiled to the lighthouse (different Act 3 staging). **Hard branch —
the Letter:** the trail ends at Dora's dresser. Open it (knowing route) or burn it
unread (willful innocence; the reveal ambushes you at the dock). Dark exit: burn
Maud's ledger instead → Ash ending.

**Act 3 — The Spring Ferry (Days 20–23).** Confession cascade: each character can
be brought to hand over their piece of the truth; each confession returns that
character's musical fragment to the mix. Then the dock, the ferry, the gangway.
Endings resolve from stat profile × Foghorn Choice × Letter Choice × confessions.

### Presence Economy (hard scarcity)
Each day offers 2–3 slots from a larger simultaneous set; ~4 of 6 confession paths
are reachable per run. Missed scenes resolve as authored **"without-you"
retellings** — biased secondhand accounts — and their motifs play faint and detuned
under the scenes you chose. NPCs track attendance ("you were at the lighthouse when
Dora needed the shutters"). FOMO calibration rule: absence must produce *good
content*, tutorialized by an early warm retelling.

### Fading is real
Presence collapse (post-Foghorn-stop decay unmanaged) triggers **Unwitnessed**
mid-Act-3. Generous autosave: reload lands 1–2 nights back.

## Endings (7, all full strength)

1. **The Sixth Bar** (true) — high UNDERTOW, all six confessions, compose an
   original sixth bar and play it on the horn as the ferry docks. Greet the real
   Wren by name; tell the town the truth; dissolve at the dock's edge — but the
   horn keeps your bar. Devastating grace.
2. **Wren, Again** — high ECHO, low UNDERTOW, Wake defended, letter burned. You
   pass. The real Wren steps off and *no one recognizes her*; she walks back up the
   gangway while the town waves at you. Quiet horror; achievement reads "Welcome
   Home."
3. **Two Wrens** (hidden) — letter opened, all confessions, high FLESH/NAME, ECHO
   deliberately capped: refuse the two harvests that erase (Dora's lullaby, Ivy's
   letter-memory). Wren reads the dock in one look and **takes your hand — the
   game's first unprompted touch.** Uncanny hope.
4. **The Stranger on the Shingle** — horn stopped, high UNDERTOW, Sam alliance;
   leave on Tam's 4 AM bus under the name you gave Maud, in Wren's seat, before the
   ferry arrives. Dawn-lit. Seeds NG+ hardest.
5. **The Long Winter** — mid stats, Maud's trust maxed, her counsel taken: one
   winter, no more. You choose your last night; Elias plays you out at 3:12; Maud
   double-inks your final entry so the book keeps you.
6. **Unwitnessed** — presence collapse. The game continues without you for four
   minutes — characters talking past your dead prompt, a ledger entry unfinished
   mid-sentence — then the interface idles at a cursor that accepts no input.
7. **Ash** — burn Maud's ledger in Act 2. It works: the town forgets Wren entirely
   — you, her, both. Dora holds the fiddle and asks Sam whose it is; Sam, promise
   horribly voided, doesn't know.

## Music — the score IS the mystery

**One broken song is the entire soundtrack.** "The Foghorn Song": six bars in
D dorian; bar 6 is a rest. Everything derives from bars 1–5; the game is,
musically, the act of reassembly, and the player finishes it or fails to.

| Character | Fragment | Voice |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Dora | bars 1–2 | music-box lullaby over harmonium |
| Elias | foghorn drone + bar 3 | the horn itself, nightly at 3:12 |
| Ivy | bar 4 **inverted** | out-of-tune pub upright; her confession un-inverts it live |
| Sam | bar 5 | whistled fast, syncopated, the way an 11-year-old sped it up |
| Maud | accompaniment chords only | never presumed to know the tune; harmonizes with everyone |
| Tam | pitchless rhythm | road-and-engine ostinato; the pulse that carried Wren out |

Mechanics: CHORD is the tracklist; STATIC detunes/bitcrushes live; ECHO adds
Wren's melodic material; UNDERTOW reharmonizes toward a new mode (the player's
candidate sixth bars haunt the underscore before they consciously compose one).
Each Act 3 confession returns a fragment to the ensemble. The reveal plays the
first complete bars 1–5 heard in the game, then holds the rest where bar 6 should
be, foghorn distant.

Adopted grafts: the **player's pub-interview fingerprint hidden (inverted) in the
main theme from the title screen**; **lie-detune** (a lying character's stem drops
a quarter-tone — CLARITY/EAR-gated listening skill, always paired with a visual
twin); **tempo-as-dread** (a fixed bpm the ear learns to associate with time
running out, deployed at the dock in every ending).

Palette: chiptune-folk hybrid — triangle music box, FM harmonium/foghorn, pulse
fiddle, noise-channel sea, tape warble. The terminal chip rendition is canon: "the
same town heard through a thinner wall."

## Prose grammar (invariants — mechanically linted)

1. **No one ever touches you first.** Every touch in the prose is initiated by
   you ("you take her hand," "you lean into the hug"). Broken exactly once, in the
   endings that break it deliberately (Two Wrens; variants at the dock).
2. **Dora never says the player-facing name** until the lullaby-taken flag.
3. **The ferry date is on screen from Day 1** (post-office pinboard scene, both
   builds).
4. Salt is described as **dry** in the Day 1 arrival text.
5. The dog **never growls** — it fails to register you.

## LLM touchpoints (4 — classification/formatting only)

The LLM reads the ledger; it never writes canonical state. Outputs are cached in
saves and never regenerated. The no-key game is complete and good.

1. **Pub Interview** — free text → stat weights + 3 self-truth tags (fixed 24-tag
   taxonomy), echoed verbatim in authored scenes. Fallback: multiple choice.
2. **Sixth Bar judging** (3 moments) — free-text attempt → enum borrowed/new.
   Fallback: notated variants pre-tagged.
3. **Ivy's Interrogation** (one scene) — answer vs fact-ledger → PASS /
   CONTRADICTION / EVASION. Fallback: dialogue-tree trap.
4. **The Last Letter** (endings 1, 3, 5) — copy-edit player's letter to period
   register, content unchanged. Fallback: authored letters.

Access: AIAdapter with three drivers — none / BYOK / edge proxy (spend-capped,
degrades to deterministic).

## NG+ / cross-run (full diegetic)

- Maud's ledger persists as a **real plain-text file beside the saves**; players
  can open it in any editor; Maud reacts if it's been edited.
- Prior run save-names surface faintly (a faded caller's name in the ledger).
- A child on the quay hums your previous run's composed sixth bar — your last self
  is town folklore now. ("How many winters? How many yous?")
- Browser and terminal share the save file; Maud acknowledges players who open
  "both doors."

## Tone & boundaries

Truly dark permitted — quiet horror, erasure, dissolution — inside warm domestic
texture (washed dishes, pub fire, morning tea). No gore; nothing gratuitous.
Landing-page content note: grief, memory loss, death of a sibling, ambiguous
self-dissolution. Accessibility: full visual parity — every audio tell has a
first-class visual twin; all 7 endings reachable silent; reduced-flicker mode.
